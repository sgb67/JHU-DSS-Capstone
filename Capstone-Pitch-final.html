Text predictor algorithms

Modern keyboards have built-in text predictor algorithms in order to ease the writing process of the user. Besides, these algorithms are also used in a variety of industries.

The objective of the Capstone Project is to predict the next word based on the preceding word(s) using a large text dataset collected from Twitter, blogs, and news (raw data: https://d396qusza40orc.cloudfront.net/dsscapstone/dataset/Coursera-SwiftKey.zip.

The Backoff with Relative Frequencies (1/2)

The algorithm developed makes use of the Backoff with Relative Frequencies or “Stupid” Backoff method. The model uses N-gram data, from bigrams to pentagrams, to assign a score to each potential prediction, which is penalised depending on the N-gram data used. The mathematical description can be seen below, where \(f\) is the frequency, \(w\) is the word and \(S\) is the score (Brants et al, 2007).

The Backoff with Relative Frequencies (2/2)

In short:

  1. Words and frequencies are added to the N-gram data.

  2. For the highest possible order N-gram data (pentagram), a score is assigned to each prediction based on the previous word frequencies as shown in the previous slide.

  3. For predictions stemming from N-gram data of decreasing order, the same is done, but the score is penalised via multiplying it by 0.4.

Shiny app

The Shiny app that enables the use of the algorithm is fairly straightforward. It can be accessed here: https://sgb67.shinyapps.io/Capstone-Project/.

It takes the text input (2 words or more) in the side panel, together with the number of predictions desired. In the main panel, a table of the top predictors along with their scores is shown.
